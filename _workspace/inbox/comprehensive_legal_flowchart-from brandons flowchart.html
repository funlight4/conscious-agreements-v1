üìÑ NEGOTIABLE INSTRUMENT (UCC 3-104)

Definition: An unconditional promise or order to pay a fixed amount of money, with or without interest or other charges described in the promise or order, if it:

  1. Is payable to bearer or to order at the time it is issued or first comes into possession of a holder
  2. Is payable on demand or at a definite time
  3. Does not state any other undertaking or instruction to do any act in addition to the payment of money
UCC ¬ß 3-104

United States v. Cooper Corp., 312 U.S. 600 (1941)

Holding: "Since, in common usage, the term 'person' does not include the sovereign, statutes employing the phrase are ordinarily construed to exclude it. But there is no hard and fast rule of exclusion. The purpose, the subject matter, the context, the legislative history, and the executive interpretation of the statute are aids to construction which may indicate an intent, by use of the term, to bring state or nation within the scope of the law."

Key Point: "Person" does not include the sovereign - the living man/woman is not automatically a "person" under statutory definitions.

Cruden v. Neale, 2 N.C. (1796) 2 S.E. 70

Holding: "It is a rule upon mankind in their natural state. There, every man is independent of all laws, except those prescribed by nature. He is not bound by any institutions formed by his fellowman without his consent."

Key Point: Natural independence exists - man is not bound by institutions without consent.

Van Valkenburg v. Brown, 43 Cal. 43 (Cal. 1872)

Holding: "No white person born within the limits of the United States and subject to their jurisdiction...owes the status of citizenship to the Constitution. The Constitution was ordained and established by the people of the United States for themselves, for their own government, and not for the government of the individual states...Those persons were not white persons, but were, in the main, persons of African descent, who had been held in slavery in this country, or having been themselves so held, were born of parents who had been so held."

Key Point: Citizenship distinctions existed pre-14th Amendment; state citizenship vs federal citizenship.

Slaughter House Cases, 83 U.S. 36 (1872)

Holdings (Multiple Points):

  1. "The 14th Amendment had created a new class of citizens - state citizenship vs United States citizenship"
  2. "No such definition was previously found in the Constitution...nor was such definition [of who was a citizen] previously found in the Constitution"
  3. "The phrase, 'subject to its jurisdiction' was intended to exclude from its operation children of ministers, consuls, and citizens or subjects of foreign States born within the United States"
  4. "It is quite clear, then, that there is a citizenship of the United States, and a citizenship of a State, which are distinct from each other"

Key Point: Two distinct types of citizenship created - critical for Private/Public distinction.

Hooven & Allison Co. v. Evatt, 324 US 652 (1945)

Holding: The term "United States" may be used in any one of several senses:

  1. As sovereignty occupying position analogous to other sovereigns
  2. As territory over which sovereignty extends
  3. As collective name of states united by Constitution

Key Point: "United States" has multiple legal meanings - context determines which applies.

County of San Luis Obispo v. Ashurst, 146 Cal. App. 3d 380 (1983)

Holding: Addresses corporation sole as "a creature established under the common law of England and is well established under corporation law in California."

Only two classes of entities may be denominated a corporation sole: religious officials or officers of religious organizations.

Key Point: Corporation sole provides unique legal status outside typical corporate structures.

Santa Clara County v. Southern Pacific Railroad, 118 U.S. 394 (1886)

Context: Referenced in discussion of 14th Amendment expansion and corporate personhood.
