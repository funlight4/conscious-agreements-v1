## EXPERT OPINION ON MONEY CREATION

### 4. Banks Do Not Lend Their Own Money or Depositors' Money

It is a common misconception that banks lend out their depositors' money or their own capital. This is categorically false. When a bank makes a loan, it creates new money through accounting entries.

### 5. The Loan Creation Process

When a borrower signs a promissory note, the bank does the following:
1. Records the promissory note as an asset on its books
2. Creates a matching deposit liability in the borrower's account
3. The deposit is new money that did not exist before
4. No money is transferred from any existing account

### 6. Federal Reserve Confirmation

The Federal Reserve has publicly acknowledged this process. In their publication "Modern Money Mechanics," they state: *"Of course, banks do not really pay out loans from the money they receive as deposits. If they did this, no additional money would be created."*

### 7. No Consideration Provided

In the traditional sense of contract law, the bank provides no consideration for the borrower's promissory note. The bank does not give up anything of value that it possessed before the transaction. Instead, it monetizes the borrower's promise to pay.

---

## THE BORROWER'S PROMISSORY NOTE

### 8. The Note as the Source of Funds

The borrower's promissory note is not merely evidence of a debt. It is the actual source of the funds that appear to be "loaned" to the borrower. The borrower's signature creates the value.

### 9. Accounting Treatment

Under Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), the bank must record the promissory note as an asset. Simultaneously, it creates a deposit liability. This is not a loan in any traditional sense - it is an exchange of assets.

### 10. The Deception

Banks do not disclose to borrowers that:
- The borrower's signature creates the money
- No pre-existing money is lent
- The bank risks nothing of its own
- The promissory note will be sold or securitized
- The transaction is an exchange, not a loan

## LEGAL IMPLICATIONS

### 11. Lack of Full Disclosure

The failure to disclose the true nature of the transaction constitutes a material omission that voids informed consent. Borrowers cannot make informed decisions without understanding that they are creating the money, not borrowing it.

### 12. Consideration in Contract Law

A valid contract requires consideration - something of value given by each party. Since the bank gives nothing of value that it possessed before the transaction, there is arguably no valid consideration, which would void the contract.

### 13. RICO Implications

The systematic nature of this deception across the banking industry, combined with the use of mail and wire communications to perpetrate it, potentially constitutes a pattern of racketeering activity under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO).

## SECURITIZATION AND MERS

### 14. Separation of Note and Mortgage

It is a fundamental principle of mortgage law that the note and mortgage must remain together. When banks securitize loans through MERS (Mortgage Electronic Registration Systems) or otherwise, they often separate the note from the mortgage, which voids the security interest.

### 15. Multiple Sales

Through securitization, the same promissory note is often sold multiple times to different investors. This is securities fraud and creates competing claims that cloud title.

### 16. Destruction of Original Notes

To hide the multiple sales and the true nature of the transactions, original promissory notes are routinely destroyed. Banks then claim the notes are "lost" when challenged to produce them.

---

## FEDERAL RESERVE OPERATIONS

### 17. The Discount Window

Banks can take borrowers' promissory notes to the Federal Reserve's discount window and exchange them for Federal Reserve Notes (currency). This proves that promissory notes have monetary value and are treated as money by the Federal Reserve System.

### 18. Fractional Reserve Lending

Through fractional reserve banking, banks can lend many times more than their deposits. A promissory note for $100,000 can result in the creation of $900,000 or more in new money through the multiplier effect.

### 19. Interest on Created Money

Banks charge interest on money they create from nothing. The borrower must repay not only the principal (which the borrower created) but also interest, which requires obtaining money created by other borrowers' debts. This makes systemic debt mathematically inevitable.
