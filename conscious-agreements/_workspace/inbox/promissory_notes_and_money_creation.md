## Part 2: The Legal Architecture

### The Unchanged 1913 Statute

Let's read 12 U.S.C. § 411, still in effect today:

> "Federal reserve notes... shall be obligations of the United States and shall be receivable by all national and member banks and Federal reserve banks and for all taxes, customs, and other public dues. **They shall be redeemed in lawful money on demand** at the Treasury Department of the United States, in the city of Washington, District of Columbia, or at any Federal Reserve bank."

This law, written in 1913, has never been updated despite fundamental changes to our monetary system.

### Critical Historical Timeline

**1913**: Federal Reserve Act passed
- Federal Reserve Notes backed by gold at $20.67/oz
- Notes were genuine promises to pay gold

**1933**: Executive Order 6102
- Citizens can no longer redeem notes for gold
- Foreign governments still can

**1971**: Nixon closes the gold window
- No one can redeem Federal Reserve Notes for gold
- But the statute still says "redeemed in lawful money"

**The Paradox**: What is "lawful money" today if not Federal Reserve Notes themselves?

## Part 3: The Mechanism Revealed

### What Actually Happens When You Get a Loan

1. **You create a Promissory Note**
   - This is YOUR promise to pay $300,000
   - You sign it (only people can make promises, not gold or dogs)
   - This note has value because of your promise to perform

2. **The Bank's Accounting Magic**
   - Bank records your Promissory Note as an **asset**
   - Bank creates a deposit (liability) of $300,000 in your account
   - The bank's books balance: $300,000 asset = $300,000 liability

3. **The Federal Reserve Discount Window**
   - Banks can "discount" your promissory note at the Fed
   - Fed creates Federal Reserve Notes based on your promise
   - Your promise has literally created the money

4. **The Stunning Conclusion**
   - You didn't borrow pre-existing money
   - Your signature created the value
   - The "loan" is actually a conversion of your promise into spendable notes

### Why It Says "Note" on Currency

A Federal Reserve Note isn't money in the traditional sense - it's a standardized, transferable representation of someone's promise to pay. That's why it still says "Note" after all these years. The terminology is accurate; it's our understanding that's been wrong.
