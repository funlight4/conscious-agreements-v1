Key Decision Points Explained

The 30-Day Window

Your strongest position is within 30 days of first contact. During this window:

  • Written dispute triggers their obligation to stop all collection
  • They must validate before resuming
  • Failure to validate = FDCPA violation

After 30 days you can still dispute, but they're not required to stop collection while validating.

What Counts as Adequate Validation

They must provide:

  • Original signed agreement ‚Äî Not just their internal records
  • Chain of assignment ‚Äî How they got authority to collect
  • Itemized accounting ‚Äî How the amount was calculated
  • Licensing proof ‚Äî Are they licensed in your state?

A computer printout or "we bought this debt" is NOT adequate validation.

Statute of Limitations

Debts expire for legal collection (typically 3-6 years depending on state and debt type):

  • After expiration, they cannot successfully sue you
  • They can still ask, but you have a complete defense
  • WARNING: Making ANY payment can restart the clock!
  • WARNING: Acknowledging the debt in writing may restart it too!

Research your state's specific statute before taking action.

If They Sue You

This is serious but not hopeless:

  • DO NOT IGNORE ‚Äî Default judgment means automatic loss
  • File your Answer before the deadline (usually 20-30 days)
  • Raise defenses: lack of standing, SOL expired, validation failure, improper service
  • Many consumer attorneys take FDCPA cases on contingency

Your FDCPA Rights

Right to Validation

Demand proof of the debt within 30 days. They must verify before continuing.

Right to Dispute

Written dispute triggers their obligation to STOP until they validate.

Right to Cease Contact

Demand they stop contacting you entirely. They must comply.

Right to Sue

Violations = $1,000 statutory damages + attorney fees.
