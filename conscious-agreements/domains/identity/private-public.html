Private vs. Public

A framework for understanding two distinct realms in the legal system: the private world of living beings with inherent rights, and the public world of legal fictions operating under statutory authority.

The Two Realms Framework

This framework proposes that there are two distinct jurisdictions operating simultaneously:

THE PRIVATE
  • Living men and women
  • Inherent/natural rights
  • Common law and equity
  • Outside federal administrative law
  • Pre-14th Amendment understanding
  • State citizens (of the several states)
THE PUBLIC
  • Legal fictions / "persons"
  • Rights granted by government
  • Statutory and administrative law
  • Under District of Columbia jurisdiction
  • Post-14th Amendment citizenship
  • "Citizens of the United States"

The Term "United States"

The Supreme Court has acknowledged that "United States" has multiple meanings:

Hooven & Allison Co. v. Evatt, 324 U.S. 652 (1945)
"The term 'United States' may be used in any one of several senses. It may be merely the name of a sovereign occupying the position analogous to that of other sovereigns in the family of nations. It may designate the territory over which the sovereignty of the United States extends, or it may be the collective name of the states which are united by and under the Constitution."

Three distinct meanings:

  1. The federal government — as a sovereign entity
  2. Federal territory — D.C., territories, federal enclaves
  3. The Union — the collective states under the Constitution

When a statute says "United States," which meaning applies? In many federal statutes, it refers primarily to federal territory — the District of Columbia and federal possessions. The states of the Union may be legally "foreign" to this "United States."

Historical Foundation

Cruden v. Neale, 2 N.C. 338 (1796)
"It is not a rule upon mankind in their natural state. There, every man is independent of all laws, except those prescribed by nature. He is not bound by any institutions formed by his fellowmen without his consent."

This early case articulates a fundamental principle: In their natural state, people are bound only by natural law — not by institutions they haven't consented to.

The question becomes: Have you consented to be bound by particular laws? And what evidence of that consent exists?

The Trust Framework

Some theorists view the relationship through a trust structure:

Trust Components
  • Grantor/Settlor/Trustor — The creator who establishes the trust and its terms
  • Trustee — The party who manages trust assets according to the terms
  • Beneficiary — The party who benefits from the trust
  • Res — The assets/property of the trust

Under this framework:

  • The living person is the grantor who created the trust (by registering the name)
  • Government agencies become trustees managing the account
  • The living person should be the beneficiary
  • The legal name/entity is the res (trust property)

A private trust would be one not operating under federal statutory law — potentially "foreign" to the "United States" in the territorial sense.

Practical Application

Recognizing Which Realm You're In

  • Court proceedings — Is this common law or statutory jurisdiction?
  • Contracts — What law governs? What definitions apply?
  • Identification — Are they addressing you or your legal entity?
  • Communications — Is your name in ALL CAPS?

Questions to Ask

  • Under what jurisdiction is this proceeding?
  • Am I the human being or the "person" as defined?
  • What is the nature of my consent to this authority?
  • What agreements have I entered (consciously or unconsciously)?

Connection to Agreements

The public realm operates on covert contracts — agreements you may have entered without realizing. Understanding the private/public distinction helps you see which agreements you may be subject to and which you may be able to challenge.

Limitations and Warnings

Critical Perspective

This framework is contested. Key limitations:

  • Court resistance — Many judges actively reject these arguments
  • "Sovereign citizen" label — Can lead to harsher treatment in legal proceedings
  • Selective acceptance — Some aspects may be valid while others are not
  • Practical power — Even if theoretically correct, enforcement may not follow

The value of this framework is primarily in understanding:

  • Seeing the system more clearly
  • Asking better questions
  • Understanding potential defenses and arguments
  • Making more informed choices about participation

It's less useful as a "magic phrase" approach to avoiding obligations. Understanding is more valuable than formulas.
