Negotiable Instruments

A negotiable instrument is a written promise or order to pay money that can be transferred from person to person. Your promissory note, a check, and a Federal Reserve Note are all negotiable instruments.

The Legal Definition

Under the Uniform Commercial Code, a negotiable instrument must meet specific requirements:

UCC § 3-104(a) — Negotiable Instrument
"Negotiable instrument" means an unconditional promise or order to pay a fixed amount of money, with or without interest or other charges described in the promise or order, if it:
  1. is payable to bearer or to order at the time it is issued or first comes into possession of a holder;
  2. is payable on demand or at a definite time; and
  3. does not state any other undertaking or instruction... to do any act in addition to the payment of money.

Every element matters. If an instrument fails any requirement, it may not be negotiable — which affects who can enforce it and what defenses apply.

The Five Requirements

1. Written and Signed

The instrument must be in writing with a signature of the maker or drawer. Oral promises don't count. The signature can be any mark intended to authenticate.

2. Unconditional Promise or Order

No "ifs" — the promise to pay must not depend on other conditions. "I promise to pay $1,000 if the goods arrive" is conditional. "I promise to pay $1,000" is unconditional.

3. Fixed Amount of Money

The amount must be determinable from the face of the instrument. Interest can be included if stated. Must be payable in money (not goods or services).

4. Payable on Demand or at Definite Time

"On demand" means whenever the holder asks. "Definite time" means a specific date or determinable period. "When I feel like it" doesn't qualify.

5. Payable to Order or Bearer

"Pay to the order of John Smith" = order paper. "Pay to bearer" or endorsed in blank = bearer paper. This determines who can present for payment.

Two Types of Instruments

UCC Article 3 recognizes two categories:

Notes Drafts
Unconditional promise to pay Unconditional order to pay
Two parties: maker and payee Three parties: drawer, drawee, payee
Examples:
  • Mortgage promissory notes
  • Federal Reserve Notes
  • Credit card transactions
  • Certificates of deposit
 Examples:
  • Checks
  • Money orders
  • Trade acceptances
  • Sight drafts
UCC § 3-104(e)
"An instrument may be a note even though it is described on its face as a check, draft, or otherwise. An instrument may be a draft even though described as a note. If an instrument falls within the definition of both 'note' and 'draft,' a person entitled to enforce the instrument may treat it as either."

Key Parties and Definitions

Maker (UCC 3-103(a)(7))

"A person who signs or is identified in a note as a person undertaking to pay."

You are the "maker" when you sign a promissory note for a mortgage.

Drawer (UCC 3-103(a)(5))

"A person who signs or is identified in a draft as a person ordering payment."

You are the "drawer" when you write a check (ordering your bank to pay).

Drawee (UCC 3-103(a)(4))

"A person ordered in a draft to make payment."

Your bank is the "drawee" when you write a check — they're ordered to pay.

Acceptor (UCC 3-103(a)(1))

"A drawee who has accepted a draft."

When the drawee agrees to pay (certifies the check), they become the acceptor.

Holder (UCC 1-201(b)(21))

"A person in possession of a negotiable instrument that is payable either to bearer or to an identified person that is the person in possession."

Whoever rightfully possesses the instrument and is entitled to payment.

Issue: When the Instrument Comes to Life

UCC § 3-105(a) — Issue
"'Issue' means the first delivery of an instrument by the maker or drawer, whether to a holder or nonholder, for the purpose of giving rights on the instrument to any person."

An instrument isn't legally active until "issued" — delivered for the purpose of creating rights. Just signing isn't enough; you must deliver it with intent.

The Significance of Issue

When you sign and deliver a promissory note to a bank, you are "issuing" a negotiable instrument. At that moment, you create something with legal value — an instrument that can be traded, sold, and used as collateral. This is the foundational act of money creation.

Promise: The Source of Value

UCC § 3-103(a)(9) — Promise
"'Promise' means a written undertaking to pay money signed by the person undertaking to pay."

Note that only a person can "undertake" — can promise. Not gold, not property, not existing money. The promise originates from human commitment.

The Critical Realization

Your promise is the source of value in a promissory note. When you sign a mortgage note, you're not just acknowledging debt — you're creating a negotiable instrument with your commitment to pay from your future earnings. This is why your signature literally creates money in the modern banking system.

Federal Reserve Notes: Government Promises

Look at a dollar bill. It says "Federal Reserve Note" at the top.

A "note" is a promise to pay. Federal Reserve Notes are negotiable instruments — the same category as your mortgage promissory note. The key differences:

  • Standardized — Uniform denominations everyone accepts
  • Legal tender — Declared acceptable for debts by statute (31 U.S.C. § 5103)
  • Bearer paper — Whoever possesses it can use it

But legally, they're the same type of instrument as your promissory note — promises to pay.

12 U.S.C. § 411
"Federal reserve notes... shall be obligations of the United States and shall be redeemed in lawful money on demand at the Treasury Department..."

This statute still says Federal Reserve Notes must be "redeemed in lawful money." If Federal Reserve Notes ARE money, what is "lawful money"? The circular logic is built into the statute.

18 U.S.C. § 8: The Overlooked Connection

18 U.S.C. § 8 — Obligation or Other Security Defined
"The term 'obligation or other security of the United States' includes all bonds, certificates of indebtedness, national bank currency, Federal Reserve notes, Federal Reserve bank notes, coupons, United States notes, Treasury notes, gold certificates, silver certificates, fractional notes, certificates of deposit, bills, checks, or drafts for money, drawn by or upon authorized officers of the United States, stamps and other representatives of value..."

This definition is remarkably broad. It includes "other representatives of value" — which some argue encompasses private promissory notes when properly negotiated into the banking system.

The implication: If your promissory note becomes a bank asset and enters the Federal Reserve system, it may become a "security of the United States" — with all the protections (and criminal penalties for fraud) that implies.

Practical Implications

When You Sign a Promissory Note

  • You are "issuing" a negotiable instrument
  • You are the "maker" — undertaking to pay
  • The instrument has value equal to the amount promised
  • It can be transferred, sold, and used as collateral
  • Your signature is the source of that value

When You Write a Check

  • You are "drawing" on your account
  • You are the "drawer" — ordering payment
  • Your bank is the "drawee" — ordered to pay
  • The payee becomes the "holder"

What This Changes

  • You're not just a "borrower" — you're the creator of the instrument
  • The bank receives something valuable from you (your note)
  • The consideration question becomes relevant: what did they give you?
  • Your understanding of "debt" shifts fundamentally
