What Is an Agreement?

Before examining specific agreements, we need clarity on what an agreement actually is — and how it differs from a command, a decree, or a wish.

The Nature of Agreement

At its simplest, an agreement is a meeting of minds — two or more parties coming together with mutual understanding and mutual consent on specific terms.

This sounds obvious. But the implications are profound:

  • Agreements require at least two parties. You cannot have an agreement with yourself. A single entity cannot impose an "agreement" unilaterally.
  • Agreements require mutual understanding. Both parties must comprehend what they're agreeing to. Hidden terms or misunderstood definitions undermine this.
  • Agreements require consent. Some form of "yes" — whether explicit or implied — must come from all parties.
Agreement
Black's Law Dictionary, 4th Edition

"A concord of understanding and intention between two or more parties with respect to the effect upon their relative rights and duties, of certain past or future facts or performances."

Notice the key elements: understanding, intention, two or more parties, and effects on rights and duties. All must be present.

Agreement vs. Command

A command is one-directional. Authority issues an instruction; subjects comply. No negotiation, no mutual consent, no meeting of minds.

Much of what appears as "law" seems like command: "You must do X. Failure results in penalty Y." But here's the crucial question: Where does the authority to command come from?

Key Insight

In most legal systems, the authority to command derives from some form of prior agreement — often one you didn't consciously make. The Constitution is an agreement. Citizenship implies agreement. Residence implies agreement. The command structure stands on agreements all the way down.

This doesn't mean commands have no force. It means their force traces back to agreements — and agreements, unlike natural law, can potentially be examined, questioned, and in some cases, exited.

The Essential Elements

For an agreement to be valid and enforceable, certain elements must be present:

1. Offer and Acceptance

One party proposes terms; another party accepts them. Without clear offer and clear acceptance, there's no agreement — just an assumption.

2. Mutual Assent (Meeting of the Minds)

Both parties must genuinely understand and intend to enter the agreement. Fraud, duress, or fundamental misunderstanding can invalidate this.

3. Consideration

Something of value must be exchanged by both parties. This is perhaps the most important element — and the most overlooked.

Consideration
Black's Law Dictionary, 4th Edition

"The inducement to a contract. The cause, motive, price, or impelling influence which induces a contracting party to enter into a contract... Some right, interest, profit, or benefit accruing to one party, or some forbearance, detriment, loss, or responsibility given, suffered, or undertaken by the other."

Critical Point

Without valid consideration — mutual exchange of value — a contract is generally void. One-sided "agreements" where only one party gives and only one party receives are not valid contracts. They may be gifts, donations, or slavery — but not enforceable contracts.

4. Lawful Purpose

The agreement must be for a purpose that isn't inherently unlawful. Agreements to commit crimes aren't enforceable.

5. Capacity

Parties must have the legal and mental capacity to enter agreements. This is why contracts with minors or the incapacitated are often voidable.

Implied vs. Express Agreements

Agreements don't always require signature on paper:

Express Agreement
Terms stated explicitly, whether in writing or verbally. Clear offer, clear acceptance, clear terms.
Implied Agreement
Terms understood from behavior, circumstances, or custom. You order food at a restaurant — implied agreement to pay. You park in a lot — implied agreement to posted terms.

Implied agreements are where most unconscious agreements live. Behaviors you didn't know constituted "acceptance." Terms you didn't know applied. Status you didn't know you had claimed.

Why This Matters

Understanding what an agreement actually is — and what it requires — gives you a framework for examining any claim of obligation:

  • Was there an offer you actually accepted?
  • Did you understand the terms (with correct definitions)?
  • Was there valid consideration both ways?
  • Did you have capacity and freedom from duress?

These questions apply to contracts, statutes, regulations, and any other claim that you "agreed" to something. The framework is universal; the applications are specific.
