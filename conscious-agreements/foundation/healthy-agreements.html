Healthy Agreements

We've examined what can go wrong with agreements. Now let's establish what a good agreement actually looks like — the standard against which all agreements can be measured.

The Purpose of This Standard

Understanding healthy agreements serves two purposes:

  1. Evaluation — You can measure existing agreements against this standard to assess their validity and fairness
  2. Creation — When you enter new agreements, you know what to aim for

A healthy agreement isn't just "legal" — it's one that both parties can genuinely benefit from and honor with integrity.

The Seven Marks of a Healthy Agreement

1. Clear Mutual Understanding

Both parties understand what they're agreeing to — using the same definitions, with no hidden terms or fine print that changes the essential nature of the deal.

Test Questions
  • Can both parties explain the agreement in plain language?
  • Do key terms mean the same thing to both parties?
  • Are there hidden clauses that materially change the deal?
  • Would either party be surprised by what the agreement actually requires?

2. Genuine Consent

Both parties enter freely, without coercion, duress, or deception. The agreement reflects actual choice, not forced acceptance.

Test Questions
  • Could either party have reasonably declined?
  • Was there pressure that made refusal effectively impossible?
  • Was accurate information provided, or was there deception?
  • Did both parties have time to consider before agreeing?

3. Valid Consideration

Both parties give and receive something of value. The exchange is mutual, not one-sided.

Test Questions
  • What is each party giving?
  • What is each party receiving?
  • Is the exchange proportional and fair?
  • Would the agreement make sense without one party's contribution?

4. Full Capacity

Both parties have the legal and mental ability to enter the agreement. They're of sound mind, legal age, and in a state to make binding decisions.

Test Questions
  • Are both parties legally able to contract?
  • Is anyone acting under diminished capacity?
  • Were any parties in altered states that affected judgment?
  • Did anyone lack the information needed to decide?

5. Lawful Purpose

The agreement serves a purpose that isn't inherently harmful or unlawful. It doesn't require either party to do wrong.

Test Questions
  • Is the purpose of the agreement legitimate?
  • Does it require anyone to harm others?
  • Does it violate natural rights or fundamental principles?
  • Would a neutral observer find the purpose reasonable?

6. Clear Exit Terms

Both parties know how to end the agreement if needed. There are defined conditions for termination, modification, or exit.

Test Questions
  • How can the agreement be ended?
  • What are the consequences of exit?
  • Is exit reasonably possible, or is it effectively impossible?
  • Are exit terms fair to both parties?

7. Balanced Consequences

The consequences for breach are proportional and apply equally. Neither party faces disproportionate penalties while the other has no accountability.

Test Questions
  • What happens if either party fails to perform?
  • Are consequences proportional to the breach?
  • Do both parties face accountability, or only one?
  • Is there a fair process for resolving disputes?

The Healthy Agreement Checklist

Before Entering Any Agreement
  1. I understand the terms — in their actual legal definitions, not just common meanings
  2. I am choosing freely — without coercion, manipulation, or artificial urgency
  3. I am receiving value — something I actually want, not just promises or "benefits" I didn't ask for
  4. I am giving value — and I'm clear on exactly what that is
  5. I can exit if needed — with clear, reasonable terms for doing so
  6. Consequences are fair — for both parties, not just me
  7. The purpose is good — or at least not harmful to myself or others

If any of these elements is missing, pause. Ask questions. Seek clarification. A healthy agreement can withstand scrutiny — if the other party resists your questions, that itself is information.

Evaluating Existing Agreements

You can apply these same standards to agreements you're already in:

  1. Identify the agreement — What obligation do you believe you have?
  2. Run through the seven marks — Does it meet each standard?
  3. Note the gaps — Which elements are missing or questionable?
  4. Assess validity — Is this a healthy agreement, or something else?
  5. Consider options — What can you do given what you've found?

Many "agreements" fail multiple tests. This doesn't automatically dissolve them, but it does give you a clearer picture of what you're dealing with.

The Key Realization

Most of what people call "obligations" don't meet the standard for healthy agreements. Once you see this, you can make more conscious choices about how to engage — whether to continue participating as before, seek modification, or explore other options.

Creating Better Agreements

When you enter new agreements, you have more control. Apply these principles:

Read Before Signing

Actually read the terms. Look up words you're not sure about — especially legal terms that may not mean what you think.

Ask Questions

If something is unclear, ask. If the other party won't clarify, that's valuable information. A party that hides information isn't approaching the agreement in good faith.

Negotiate Terms

Most agreements are negotiable, even if presented as "standard." Push back on terms that seem unfair. If the other party refuses all modification, consider whether you want to proceed.

Document Understanding

Put your understanding in writing. If there's a dispute later about what was agreed, your documentation matters.

Reserve Your Rights

When signing under circumstances less than ideal, consider reserving your rights using appropriate language. This isn't a magic phrase, but it can help preserve options.
