Money Creation

Understanding how money is actually created reveals why consideration matters so profoundly — and why most "debts" may not be what they appear.

The Key Insight

When you sign a promissory note for a loan, you're not borrowing pre-existing money. Your signature creates the money. The bank converts your promise into spendable currency — then claims you owe them for your own creation.

This isn't conspiracy theory. It's documented in Federal Reserve publications, banking textbooks, and federal statutes. The Bank of England openly states: "Commercial banks create money, in the form of bank deposits, by making new loans."

Understanding this changes everything about how you view debt, mortgages, and the consideration question in banking agreements.

Why This Matters

This isn't abstract economics. It directly affects the agreements you're in:

  • Mortgages — If your signature created the $300,000, why do you "owe" it?
  • Credit Cards — Each swipe creates new money through your promise
  • Car Loans — Same principle, smaller scale
  • Student Loans — Your future labor, monetized before you begin

In each case, the traditional story says: "The bank has money, they lend it to you, you pay it back." But the documented reality is: "You create a promise, the bank monetizes it, then claims you owe them."

The Consideration Question

Valid contracts require consideration from both parties. You provide your promise (real consideration). But what does the bank provide? If they simply convert YOUR promise into spendable form, where is THEIR consideration?

This question has been raised in courts, largely unsuccessfully — but understanding it gives you a different lens on what "debt" actually is.
