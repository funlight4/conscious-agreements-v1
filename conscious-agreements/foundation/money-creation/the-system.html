The Consideration Paradox

Every contract requires consideration from both parties. If the bank creates money from YOUR promise, what consideration do THEY provide? This is the legal vulnerability at the heart of modern banking.

The Foundation of Contract Law

Every first-year law student learns: a valid contract requires consideration from both parties.

Consideration
Black's Law Dictionary, 4th Edition

"Something of value given in exchange for a promise; the inducement to enter a contract; a benefit to the promisor or detriment to the promisee."

Three requirements for valid consideration:

  1. Must be bargained for — Not a gift or past action
  2. Must have legal value — Even if minimal
  3. Must be real, not illusory — Actually given up or promised

Without valid consideration from both parties, there is no valid contract.

The Consideration Analysis

Let's apply this test to a modern mortgage:

  • Promissory note worth $300,000
  • Legal obligation to pay principal + interest
  • Pledge of future labor/productivity
  • Security interest in the property
 Yes
Real, bargained-for value
Bank (Lender)
  • Bookkeeping entries
  • Account management
  • Processing services
  • Access to your own monetized promise
 ?
If money came from YOUR note, where is THEIR consideration?
The Paradox

The bank is essentially:

  1. Taking your promissory note (worth $300,000)
  2. Recording it as an asset (like depositing a check)
  3. Creating a deposit in "your" account
  4. Claiming you owe them $300,000 plus interest

But you already GAVE them $300,000 — in the form of your promissory note. What did THEY give up?

Historical Contrast

Before 1971 (Gold Standard)

  • Bank gives: Gold-backed currency (real pre-existing value)
  • Borrower gives: Promise to repay
  • Valid mutual consideration

After 1971 (Current System)

  • Bank gives: Credit entries created from YOUR promise
  • Borrower gives: Promise to repay
  • One-sided consideration?

The fundamental change in how money works post-1971 wasn't accompanied by any change in how loan contracts are understood. We're using 19th-century contract assumptions for a 21st-century monetary reality.

Why Courts Don't Accept This

This argument has been raised in courts. It almost never succeeds. Here's why:

Layer 1: Complexity Obscures Truth
If people can't understand it, they can't challenge it.

Multiple intermediary steps hide the simple truth. Technical jargon confuses laypeople. Even lawyers rarely understand banking mechanics. Judges often lack financial expertise.

Layer 2: Procedural Dismissals
Never reach the substantive question.

Courts avoid the consideration question through standing requirements, jurisdiction issues, procedural defects, and "frivolous lawsuit" doctrine.

Layer 3: Holder in Due Course
Legal immunity through commercial law.

Under UCC Article 3, banks become "holders in due course" — a status that provides immunity from most defenses, including lack of consideration claims.

Layer 4: Judicial Notice
Predetermined conclusions prevent examination.

Courts take "judicial notice" that banks lend money, that this is how banking works, that Federal Reserve Notes are money. Challenging this is dismissed as "frivolous."

Layer 5: Legal Tender Laws
Statutory override of common law.

31 U.S.C. § 5103 declares Federal Reserve Notes as legal tender. Courts interpret this as meaning FRNs are valid consideration by law — regardless of how they're created.

Layer 6: Systemic Protection
"Too big to fail" applies to legal challenges too.

Even if the argument were accepted, courts face: "This would collapse the economy." "Too disruptive to remedy." "Congressional action needed." Pragmatism overrides law.

The Credit River Decision

One case did accept this argument:

First National Bank of Montgomery v. Daly (1969) — Known as the "Credit River Decision"

Justice Martin V. Mahoney ruled:

  • The bank admitted they created credit "out of nothing"
  • No lawful consideration was provided by the bank
  • The contract was void for lack of consideration
  • The foreclosure was invalid

The bank's president testified: "The money and credit first came into existence when they created it."

Result: The decision was buried, declared to have no precedential value, and largely disappeared from legal databases. Justice Mahoney died six months later under disputed circumstances.

This is not cited as proof of conspiracy — but as an example of what happens when the system's fundamental assumptions are challenged.

What Actually Works

Direct attacks on the consideration question rarely succeed. What has worked:

Procedural Challenges

  • Chain of title issues — Who actually owns the note?
  • Standing challenges — Does the plaintiff have the right to sue?
  • MERS problems — Electronic registration system defects
  • Robo-signing — Document fraud in foreclosure paperwork

Statutory Violations

  • TILA violations — Truth in Lending Act disclosure failures
  • RESPA violations — Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act issues
  • FDCPA violations — Fair Debt Collection Practices Act breaches

Resolution Paths

  • Loan modifications — Negotiated within the system
  • Short sales — Agreed exits
  • Bankruptcy — Legal discharge mechanism
  • Quiet title actions — Make them prove ownership
Strategic Reality

Attack the procedure, not the system. Use existing statutes. Make them prove their case. The consideration argument provides understanding — but practical success usually comes through other routes.

Why This Still Matters

Even if the consideration paradox doesn't win in court, understanding it matters:

  1. Clarity — You understand what you're actually in
  2. Leverage — You negotiate from knowledge, not fear
  3. Questions — You know what to ask, what to challenge
  4. Choices — You see the system clearly and can choose consciously

The system depends on people not understanding it. Once you understand the consideration paradox, you can't unsee it — and that changes how you navigate.

Paths Forward

1
Work Within the System

Use the knowledge for negotiation leverage. Pursue modifications, settlements, or statutory challenges. Pragmatic but effective.

2
Minimize Participation

Reduce debt-based agreements. Use credit unions or public banks. Live within means that don't require creating money through loans.

3
Support Alternatives

Public banking initiatives. Monetary reform movements. Educational campaigns. Change the system over time.

4
Document and Prepare

Understand your specific agreements. Know the questions to ask. Be ready to challenge when appropriate. Knowledge is protection.
